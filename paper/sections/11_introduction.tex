\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}
Chemical substances are central entities in environmental, health, and product regulation. Across European legislation, substances are used to define obligations, assess risks, and communicate hazards in frameworks such as the Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals~(REACH)~\cite{REACH2006}, the Classification, Labelling and Packaging of chemicals~(CLP)~\cite{CLP2008}, and sector-specific regulations including the EU Pesticides Regulation~\cite{EC2009PesticidesReg} and the Water Framework Directive~\cite{EC2000WFD}. Effective governance of these substances increasingly depends on the ability to integrate and reuse data across regulatory domains.

In chemistry and cheminformatics, chemical identity is well-defined and operationalised through algorithmically defined, structure-based, representations. Molecular structures can be uniquely encoded using standards such as SMILES notations~\cite{Weininger1988}, IUPAC names~\cite{Koppenol2016}, the International Chemical Identifier~(InChI)~\cite{Heller2015} and its hashed representation, the InChIKey, enabling unambiguous identification, comparison, and linking of substances across databases~\cite{Akhondi2012}. Large-scale infrastructures such as PubChem~\cite{Kim2015} and ChEBI~\cite{Degtyarenko2008} build on these identifiers to provide machine-readable, ontology-based knowledge graphs that support interoperability and automated reasoning.

In contrast, regulatory data systems adopt a fundamentally different approach. Substances are typically identified using heterogeneous and often ambiguous identifiers, including Chemical Abstracts Service~(CAS) registry numbers~\cite{CAS,Dittmar1976}, European Community numbers~(EC-numbers)~\cite{ECHA_EC_numbers}, textual names, and informal group descriptions. Moreover, the concept of a \emph{substance} in regulation is not uniform: it may refer to a single molecule, a mixture of unknown or variable composition~(UVCB)~\cite{Kutsarova2019}, a group of related compounds (e.g.,~PFAS), or even an analytical parameter defined as a function over multiple substances.
Figure~\ref{fig:mismatch} illustrates this contrast: the left panel shows the chemistry view, in which a substance corresponds to a molecule unambiguously identified by structure-derived identifiers such as InChI or InChIKey; the right panel shows the regulatory view, where the same term encompasses heterogeneous entities of fundamentally different semantic types.

As a result, integrating substance information across regulatory frameworks becomes a complex and largely manual task, hindering policy enforcement for environmental administrations such as the Flemish Department of Environment and Spatial Development~\cite{InteroperableEuropeAct2024}, and increasing uncertainty for industrial players in environmental management, investment, and innovation.
Figure~\ref{fig:regulatory_groups} illustrates this concretely using PFAS as an example: individual molecules such as PFOS, PFBA, and PFBS are grouped into a regulatory substance group without explicit set-membership semantics, while an analytical parameter such as \emph{Sum PFOS + PFBA} is a derived entity defined as a function over individual members.
Neither relationship is formally specified, leading to ambiguity and combinatorial complexity during cross-dataset harmonisation.

This paper investigates the extent and consequences of this interoperability problem using a comprehensive analysis of 18 regulatory datasets from the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) and related sources. We focus on three main research questions:

\begin{itemize}
	
	\item To what extent can substances in European regulatory datasets be linked to a precise, unique chemical structure?
	\label{sec:question1}
	\item How consistent and reliable are commonly used identifiers such as CAS numbers for cross-dataset linking?
	\label{sec:question2}
	\item \looseness=-1 Can semantic or embedding-based methods compensate for the absence of structure-based chemical identifiers?
	\label{sec:question3}
\end{itemize}

Based on this analysis, we make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item We quantify the heterogeneity of regulatory substance definitions and introduce a taxonomy of linkability reflecting their degree of chemical specificity.
	\label{sec:contribution1}
	\item \looseness=-1 We demonstrate the limitations of CAS-based identification and show that a substantial fraction of regulatory substances cannot be resolved to a uniquely defined molecular structure.
	\label{sec:contribution2}
	\item We evaluate semantic embedding approaches and show that they fail to recover chemical identity for non-structure substances.
	\label{sec:contribution3}
	\item We estimate the scale of the manual effort required for retrospective harmonisation, demonstrating its infeasibility.
	\label{sec:contribution4}
	\item We show that structure-based identifiers combined with ontology-driven approaches enable scalable integration through knowledge graphs.
	\label{sec:contribution5}
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the data sources and analytical methods. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the empirical findings. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the implications for regulatory data infrastructures and semantic interoperability. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with recommendations for future work.


\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Interoperability of scientific data has been extensively studied in the context of FAIR principles~\cite{Wilkinson2016} and knowledge graph infrastructures. In the life sciences, large-scale resources such as PubChem~\cite{Kim2015} and the Chemical Entities of Biological Interest (ChEBI) ontology~\cite{Degtyarenko2008} demonstrate how chemical entities can be represented using structure-based identifiers, ontology-driven classifications, and machine-readable knowledge graphs, enabling consistent integration, querying, and reuse of chemical information across scientific, environmental, and regulatory domains. Rule-based chemical classification is further exemplified by ClassyFire~\cite{DjoumbouFeunang2016}, which establishes a structure-based taxonomy (ChemOnt) in which each class is characterised by precise and unambiguous structural rules.

Within the Semantic Web community, numerous approaches have been proposed for data integration based on linked data principles, ontology alignment, and entity resolution. Techniques such as identifier mapping, schema matching, and, more recently, embedding-based similarity methods~\cite{Reimers2019} have been applied to address heterogeneity across datasets. However, these approaches typically assume that entities correspond to well-defined and comparable concepts.

In cheminformatics and regulatory science, considerable effort has focused on handling substances without precise structural definitions, such as UVCBs. Methods like those of Kutsarova et al.~\cite{Kutsarova2019} use generic structural encodings (e.g., G SMILES) and statistical sampling to generate representative constituent structures, reducing combinatorial complexity while preserving structural diversity.

These approaches operate at the level of individual substances and do not address broader semantic inconsistencies across regulatory datasets. Many entries still lack structure-resolvable identifiers or rely on heterogeneous definitions, limiting interoperability between data sources.

\looseness=-1 In the regulatory domain, previous work has highlighted challenges related to data fragmentation, heterogeneity of identifiers, and limited machine-readability of legal data~\cite{Filtz2021}. In particular, chemical regulation introduces additional complexity due to the use of substance groups, mixtures, and analytical parameters that do not correspond to single chemical structures. Closer to the regulatory domain, Sepehri et al.~\cite{Sepehri2025TOXIN} developed the TOXIN knowledge graph, which integrates toxicological data for EU cosmetic ingredient safety assessment using ontology-driven semantic technology, demonstrating that such approaches are applicable to regulatory chemical data. However, these approaches address single-domain integration and do not characterise the broader semantic mismatch across regulatory datasets. While prior studies have focused on improving data accessibility or applying FAIR principles to regulatory datasets, the fundamental mismatch between chemical identity and regulatory substance definitions has not been systematically characterised. The present study addresses this gap.
