\begin{abstract}
Chemical substances are fundamental entities in environmental and chemical regulation. However, the representation and identification of substances in regulatory information systems often diverge significantly from established practices in chemistry and cheminformatics. While chemical identity can be unambiguously defined using structure-based identifiers such as the International Chemical Identifier and its hashed form, the InChIKey, regulatory datasets typically rely on heterogeneous identifiers including CAS numbers, EC numbers, textual names, and informal group descriptions.

This paper analyses the interoperability challenges arising from this discrepancy across European regulatory frameworks, including datasets derived from environmental monitoring and lists of substances of concern. Using a case study of Flemish environmental data infrastructure, we demonstrate that regulatory "substances" frequently represent heterogeneous entities, including individual chemical compounds, mixtures, analytical sum parameters, and loosely defined substance groups. As a consequence, the semantic relationships between these entities—such as equality, subset relations, or partial overlap—are rarely explicitly defined.

We show that the absence of formal chemical identifiers, ontologies, and structure-based representations leads to significant combinatorial complexity when attempting to align substance lists across regulatory contexts. In contrast, cheminformatics infrastructures such as PubChem and ChEBI provide structure-based identifiers, ontological classification, and machine-readable knowledge graphs. We argue that integrating such approaches into regulatory data infrastructures is essential for achieving interoperable and FAIR chemical substance data in Europe.
\end{abstract}
