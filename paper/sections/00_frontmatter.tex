\title{Chemical Identity Lost in Regulation: A Study of Semantic Interoperability in European Chemical Substance Data}
\runningtitle{Chemical Identity Lost in Regulation}
%\subtitle{Subtitle}

\begin{abstract}
% Context
Chemical substances appear across European environmental regulations, but regulatory datasets often represent them using heterogeneous identifiers and semantically mixed substance categories.
% Need
This creates a major interoperability gap, as it remains unclear to what extent regulatory substance records can be linked to defined chemical structures across datasets.
% Task
We analysed 18 ECHA and related datasets by mapping entries to InChIKeys where possible, classifying records by linkability, testing the consistency of CAS-number mappings, evaluating embedding-based semantic matching, and estimating the effort required for manual reconciliation and analysed whether commonly used identifiers or semantic methods can compensate for this mismatch.
% Object
This paper presents a case study of semantic interoperability in European chemical substance data and a knowledge-graph-based analysis of how regulatory substance identity is represented across regulatory datasets.
% Findings
We found that only 62.5\% of entries could be directly linked to a chemical structure, that CAS numbers do not function as reliably unique identifiers, that embedding-based approaches fail to recover chemical identity for non-structure substances, and that retrospective harmonisation would be infeasible at scale.
% Conclusion
These results show that interoperable and FAIR chemical regulation requires structure-based identifiers, formal ontologies, and machine-readable data models embedded at the level of regulatory design.
As resource nonetheless, we include the scripts to generate an integrated knowledge graph from 18 regulatory sources.

\end{abstract}
