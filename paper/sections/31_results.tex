\subsection{Dataset Overview}

Integration of 18 regulatory sources yielded 41,813 records. Of these,
63\% could be linked to a defined molecular structure via InChIKey.


\subsection{Heterogeneity of Substance Definitions}
\label{sec:semantic_mismatch}

A substantial fraction of regulatory entries does not correspond to a well-defined chemical structure. Approximately 37\% of entries are non-structure substances, including mixtures (UVCBs), substance groups, analytical parameters, or entries lacking usable identifiers. This heterogeneity reflects fundamental differences in how “substances” are defined across regulatory contexts (cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:regulatory_groups}).

The degree of heterogeneity varies strongly between regulatory instruments: some lists are largely structure-based, while others are dominated by group or mixture definitions, limiting their interoperability.

Application of the seven-tier linkability taxonomy (Section~\ref{sec:linkability}) to the full dataset reveals that 20,909 entries (62.5\%) are fully addressable via a direct InChIKey (tier 1; Figure~\ref{fig:analysis4d}). A further 60 entries correspond to substance groups that can be partially resolved: 15 entries (${<}0.1$\%) belong to tier 2 (group with resolvable base compound) and 45 (0.1\%) to tier 3 (group with unresolvable base compound). Together, these tiers represent the structure-addressable portion of the dataset.

The remaining 37.5\% of entries are not directly addressable by structure. Among these, 3,865 entries (11.6\%) carry a CAS number but cannot be resolved to a unique InChIKey (tier 4), demonstrating that CAS identifiers do not guarantee structural identity. A further 3,622 entries (10.8\%) correspond to UVCB substances, undefined mixtures, or analytical parameters (tier 5), for which no single molecular representation exists. Additionally, 15 entries (${<}0.1$\%) represent administrative or regulatory constructs (tier 6), and 4,981 entries (14.9\%) lack any actionable identifier (tier 7). This final group — nearly one in six regulatory records — constitutes the most significant barrier to automated cross-dataset substance matching.

These results show that more than one-third of regulatory substance entries cannot be linked to a well-defined chemical structure, and that even commonly used identifiers such as CAS numbers fail to ensure unambiguous identification. As a result, structure-based integration approaches can only be applied to a subset of regulatory data, while the remainder requires fundamentally different modelling strategies.

\subsection{Limitations of CAS Numbers}

Although CAS numbers are widely used, they do not provide a reliable
unique identifier. Multiple CAS numbers may refer to the same structure,
and conversely, a single CAS number may correspond to multiple structures.
This confirms that CAS-based linking is insufficient for interoperability.

\subsection{Limited Interoperability Across Regulatory Lists}

Cross-list analysis shows that most substances are unique to a single
regulatory source. Overlap between lists is limited, reflecting the
domain-specific design of regulatory instruments rather than a shared
chemical reference framework.

\subsection{Failure of Semantic Approaches for Non-Structure Substances}
Embedding-based clustering of non-structure substance names yields semantically coherent groups, but does not resolve chemical identity. While clusters reflect lexical or contextual similarity between substance descriptions, they do not correspond to well-defined molecular entities.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_9n_quality_landscape.pdf}
	\caption{Match quality landscape: score gap $\Delta_{12}$ versus best match score $s_1$ for all non-structure substance--ChemOnt class pairs. Entries in the upper-right region satisfy both quality thresholds and are retained as high-confidence matches.}
	\label{fig:analysis9n}
\end{figure}




To assess whether semantic similarity can be used to recover chemical meaning, we attempted to match non-structure substances to chemical taxonomy classes (ChemOnt). High-confidence matches were obtained for less than 1\% of cases, indicating that semantic approaches are largely insufficient for reconstructing chemical identity.

The match quality landscape (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis9n}) plots the score gap $\Delta_{12}$ between the best and second-best matches against the best match score $s_1$ for all substance--class pairs. Entries in the upper-right region (high $s_1$, high $\Delta_{12}$) correspond to unambiguous matches and represent the 107 high-confidence mappings retained in our analysis. In contrast, entries with high $s_1$ but low $\Delta_{12}$ indicate genuine ambiguity between multiple candidate classes, reflecting the underspecified nature of the original substance definitions.

The predominance of low-confidence and ambiguous matches demonstrates that semantic and embedding-based methods cannot compensate for the absence of structure-based identifiers. In particular, these approaches fail systematically for substance groups, mixtures, and analytical parameters, where no single underlying chemical structure exists.





\subsection{Scale of the Interoperability Problem}
For the more than 15,000 non-structure entries, full manual reconciliation would require on the order of hundreds of millions of pairwise comparisons. Under conservative assumptions, this corresponds to several thousand person-years of expert effort.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_10c_workload_non_structured.pdf}
	\caption{
		Estimated pairwise curation workload for 15,432 non-structure entries across the combined regulatory dataset, broken down by source. 
		The total corresponds to approximately 1.2 million person-days (nearly 6,000 person-years) under an assumed throughput of 100 pair assessments per person-day. 
		The quadratic growth of the workload illustrates that manual reconciliation is not scalable, reinforcing the need for structure-based and ontology-driven representations at the source.
	}
	\label{fig:analysis10c}
\end{figure}



Figure~\ref{fig:analysis10c} quantifies this workload across all regulatory sources, illustrating both the absolute scale and the quadratic growth that makes each additional non-structure entry disproportionately costly. Even moderate increases in the number of unresolved entries lead to a rapidly escalating curation burden.

Manual pairwise reconciliation at this scale is not operationally feasible. This result highlights a fundamental constraint: interoperability cannot be achieved through retrospective harmonisation of heterogeneous substance definitions.

Instead, the only scalable strategy is to address the problem at its source. By requiring structure-resolvable identifiers and ontology-based representations in regulatory definitions wherever chemically applicable, the number of entries requiring manual reconciliation can be structurally reduced rather than managed post hoc.


\subsection{Knowledge Graph Integration}

Structure-defined substances can be successfully integrated across
regulatory lists, chemical taxonomies (ChemOnt), and biological
annotations (ChEBI) using InChIKey as a linking key. This demonstrates
the feasibility of a linked-data approach when structure information is
available.



\subsection{Priority Patterns}

The integrated knowledge graph enables the combination of regulatory 
and biological annotations into a shared analytical space. 
Using the composite priority score defined in Section~\ref{sec:priority_score}, 
we identify substances and chemical classes that are prominent across 
both regulatory presence and biological role annotations.

The bubble chart (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis14e}) visualises this 
priority space by plotting $\text{list\_score}$ against 
$\text{bio\_score}$ for all scored substances. 
Bubble size is proportional to the number of regulatory source memberships. 


\begin{figure}[!ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_14e_Bubble_priority.pdf}
	\caption{Bubble chart of composite priority space: regulatory list score versus biological hazard score for all scored substances. Bubble size is proportional to the number of regulatory source memberships. Substances in the upper-right quadrant combine broad regulatory coverage with documented biological hazard evidence.}
	\label{fig:analysis14e}
\end{figure}

Substances located in the upper-right quadrant combine high regulatory 
coverage with multiple biological role annotations, reflecting their 
prominence across heterogeneous data sources. 
Examples include bisphenol~A (BPA) and dibutyl phthalate, which appear 
consistently across regulatory lists and are associated with multiple 
biological roles in ChEBI.

Importantly, this analysis does not constitute a toxicological ranking, 
but demonstrates how interoperable data representations enable the 
integration of regulatory and ontology-based information within a 
single analytical framework.

At the class level, a small number of chemical classes accounts for 
a large fraction of regulated substances. This concentration suggests 
that class-based representations can provide a scalable bridge between 
individual substances and regulatory group definitions, supporting 
alignment across regulatory contexts.
