\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Data Sources and Integration}
\label{sec:results-datasources}

Integration of the 18 regulatory sources (see Section~\ref{sec:datasources}) yielded a unified dataset of 41,813 regulatory records. Each record was linked to its originating source and enriched with all available chemical identifiers (CAS number, EC number, substance name, and InChIKey where resolvable). The combined dataset spans the full breadth of European regulatory instruments, from broadly scoped REACH registration data (\textit{reach\_registrations}, 25,200 records) to narrowly focused obligation lists such as the SVHC identification list (273 records) and the POPs process list (12 records). The Flemish \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} list contributes a further 1,390 records dominated by substance groups without resolved structures.

\subsection{Chemical Identity Classification}
\label{sec:results-classification}

The rule-based classifier assigned each of the 41,813 regulatory records to one of seven mutually exclusive entity types. The majority — 26,376 records (63.1\%) — are classified as \textit{Molecule}. The next largest categories are \textit{Unclassified} (6,359; 15.2\%), \textit{CAS without structure} (4,851; 11.6\%), and \textit{Analytical parameter} (3,440; 8.2\%). The remaining categories are comparatively small: \textit{Mixture} (685; 1.6\%), \textit{Substance group} (86; 0.2\%), and \textit{Regulatory entry} (16; 0.04\%).

Figure~\ref{fig:analysis4b} illustrates how the entity type composition varies substantially across individual regulatory sources. Sources rooted in pharmaceutical and pesticide authorisation contexts — ED Assessment (85.5\% Molecule), CLH Process (84.2\%), and the Candidate List (80.6\%) — are dominated by structure-defined entries. In contrast, the \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} list (3.9\% Molecule) and the Restriction Process (29.4\%) contain a high proportion of unclassified or group-type entries, reflecting the broader, less chemically specific scope of those instruments.

\subsection{Linkability Taxonomy}
\label{sec:results-linkability}

Application of the seven-tier linkability taxonomy (Section~\ref{sec:linkability}) to the full dataset reveals that 20,909 entries (62.5\%) are fully addressable via a direct InChIKey (tier 1; Figure~\ref{fig:analysis4d}). A further 60 substance group entries could be partially resolved: 15 entries (${<}0.1$\%) belong to tier 2 (group with resolvable base compound) and 45 (0.1\%) to tier 3 (group with unresolvable base compound). Together these tiers comprise the structure-addressable portion of the dataset.

The remaining 37.5\% of entries are not directly addressable by structure. Of these, 3,865 entries (11.6\%) carry a CAS number but no resolvable InChIKey (tier 4); 3,622 (10.8\%) are UVCB substances or undefined mixtures (tier 5); 15 entries (${<}0.1$\%) are administrative regulatory entries (tier 6); and 4,981 (14.9\%) are fully unidentified entries with no actionable identifier (tier 7). This last group — nearly one in six regulatory records — represents the most significant barrier to automated cross-list substance matching.

\subsection{Identifier Mapping Consistency}
\label{sec:results-identifier-mapping}

Of the 41,813 regulatory records, 17,277 carry both a CAS number and an InChIKey and could be assessed for bidirectional mapping consistency (Section~\ref{sec:identifier-mapping}).

In the CAS$\rightarrow$InChIKey direction, 17,273 of 17,277 assessed CAS numbers (99.98\%) map unambiguously to a single InChIKey. The remaining four CAS numbers each map to exactly two distinct InChIKeys. These discrepancies arise from entries in which the same CAS registry number has been assigned to structurally related but distinct compounds — most commonly stereoisomeric pairs, hydrate/anhydrous forms, or alternative salt representations recorded in different regulatory dossiers.

In the InChIKey$\rightarrow$CAS direction, 17,074 unique InChIKeys could be assessed, of which 16,879 (98.9\%) are associated with a single CAS number. The remaining 195 InChIKeys (1.1\%) each map to two or more CAS numbers (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis3b}), reflecting the common regulatory practice of assigning separate registry numbers to the same molecule under different trade, salt, or regulatory-context identifiers. These findings confirm that CAS numbers alone are insufficient as unambiguous primary identifiers for cross-list substance linking, and motivate the use of InChIKey as the primary key throughout subsequent analyses.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_3b_CAS-InChIKey_inconsistencies_multiple_CAS_numbers.pdf}
  \caption{Distribution of the number of distinct CAS numbers per InChIKey across 17,074 assessed structures. One hundred and ninety-five InChIKeys are associated with two or more CAS numbers, indicating registry fragmentation.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Structure Coverage and Linking Ratio}
\label{sec:results-coverage}

Across the combined dataset, 26,376 of 41,813 regulatory records (63.1\%) are structure-defined, i.e.\ possess a valid InChIKey. The 20,909 unique InChIKeys thus identified represent the directly linkable chemical space within European regulatory legislation.

Structure coverage varies substantially across the 18 individual sources (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis2}), ranging from 85.5\% (ED Assessment) to 3.9\% (\textit{sommatie\_stoffen}). Sources operating within tightly defined pharmaceutical or pesticide authorisation frameworks exhibit the highest coverage: ED Assessment (85.5\%), CLH Process (84.2\%), Candidate List (80.6\%), and SVHC Identification List (79.5\%). The large REACH Registrations dataset, which contributes 25,200 records — more than 60\% of the total — achieves 63.3\% coverage. Sources with the lowest coverage include the Restriction Process (29.4\%), the POPs Process (33.3\%), and \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} (3.9\%), the latter dominated by substance group entries for which individual molecular structures are not specified.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering

  \caption{Structure coverage (InChIKey availability) broken down by regulatory source. Sources are ordered by decreasing coverage. The 18-fold range from 85.5\% (ED Assessment) to 3.9\% (\textit{sommatie\_stoffen}) reflects fundamental differences in regulatory scope and the chemical specificity of substance identity requirements.}
  \label{fig:analysis2}
\end{figure}

The log-log scatter plot of records versus unique InChIKeys per source (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis6}) illustrates the per-source linking ratio. Sources plotting near the 1:1 diagonal (e.g.\ the POPs list, Candidate List) show a near-one-to-one correspondence between regulatory records and distinct molecular structures. Sources well below the diagonal — notably REACH Registrations, Harmonised Classification List, and Dossier Evaluation — contain multiple records mapping to the same InChIKey, reflecting either systematic structural redundancy (e.g.\ separate entries for different salt forms of the same compound) or a high fraction of non-structure entries that suppress the effective ratio.

\subsection{Cross-List Substance Overlap}
\label{sec:results-overlap}

The UpSet plot in Figure~\ref{fig:analysis5} illustrates the pattern of structure-defined substance overlap across all sources with at least ten unique InChIKeys. The dominant pattern is source-specificity: the largest intersection bars correspond to substances appearing in only a single regulatory list. Shared substances across multiple lists are substantially less frequent, and the shared core across three or more lists is comparatively small. This distribution reflects the domain-specific design of individual regulatory instruments, each targeting a distinct subset of the chemical landscape rather than a common universe of regulated compounds.

\subsection{Semantic Embedding and Clustering of Non-Structure Substances}
\label{sec:results-clustering}

Silhouette score analysis across $k \in \{2, \ldots, 12\}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis8b}) identifies $k = 6$ as the optimal number of k-means clusters for the sentence-transformer embeddings of the 15,432 non-structure substance names (Section~\ref{sec:clustering}). The silhouette score peaks at $k = 6$ before declining monotonically for larger $k$, confirming that six clusters provide the best trade-off between cohesion and separation.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Analysis_8b_silhouette_score.pdf}
  \caption{Silhouette score as a function of the number of k-means clusters $k$ for sentence-embedding-based clustering of non-structure substance names. The score peaks at $k = 6$.}
  \label{fig:analysis8b}
\end{figure}

The UMAP projection (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis8d}) shows clearly separated cluster regions, confirming that the six groups are semantically coherent. Manual inspection of cluster contents yielded the following labels:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textit{Reaction masses} — defined isomer or intermediate mixtures described by the ``Reaction mass of \ldots'' naming convention;
  \item \textit{Complex UVCB substances} — multi-constituent or variable compositions not reducible to a single structure;
  \item \textit{Petroleum and coal tar fractions} — distillation cuts, refinery streams, and tar derivatives;
  \item \textit{Inorganic compounds and metal salts} — simple inorganic species, oxides, and coordination compounds;
  \item \textit{Trade names, codes, and biological materials} — commercial product identifiers and biological matrix entries;
  \item \textit{Polymers, fatty acids, and surfactants} — polymeric substances, long-chain fatty acids, and amphiphilic compounds.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_8d_UMAP.pdf}
  \caption{UMAP projection of sentence-transformer embeddings of non-structure substance names, coloured by k-means cluster assignment ($k = 6$). Each cluster corresponds to a semantically coherent chemical or regulatory category.}
  \label{fig:analysis8d}
\end{figure}

These clusters illustrate that semantic embeddings can organise non-structure substance names into coherent groups, but cannot resolve their chemical identity or establish the formal ontological relationships needed for cross-list interoperability.

\subsection{Chemical Taxonomy Matching}
\label{sec:results-chemont}

\subsubsection{Non-Structure Substances}

Of the 15,432 non-structure substance names, only 107 (0.7\%) received a high-confidence ChemOnt assignment ($s_1 > 0.8$, $\Delta_{12} > 0.05$). The empirical cumulative distribution of best-match scores (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis9g}) shows that approximately half of all non-structure substance names achieve a cosine similarity below 0.5 against any ChemOnt class label, confirming that embedding-based taxonomy matching cannot substitute for structure-based chemical identification for compositional, trade-name, and UVCB-type entries.

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Construction}
\label{sec:results-kg}

All processed data — linkability taxonomy assignments, k-means cluster labels, ClassyFire direct-parent annotations, and ChEBI role annotations — were serialised to a unified RDF Turtle knowledge graph as described in Section~\ref{sec:kg}. The graph integrates substance-level nodes (one per unique InChIKey or substance name), their regulatory source memberships, chemical taxonomy classifications, and biological role annotations. Semantic enrichment via SPARQL CONSTRUCT queries over the merged ChemOnt 2.1 SKOS ontology and ChEBI OWL graph enables downstream SPARQL-based queries across the regulatory, structural, and biological dimensions of the dataset. The output file \texttt{data/processed/rdf/substances\_taxonomy.ttl} constitutes the machine-readable foundation for the priority scoring analysis described in the following subsection.

\subsection{Workload Estimation for Manual Curation}
\label{sec:results-workload}

Across the full combined ECHA dataset, 15,432 regulatory entries lack a resolvable chemical structure (Section~\ref{sec:results-classification}).  Establishing the pairwise semantic relationships --- subset, overlap, or equivalence --- among these entries requires expert assessment of every unique pair, yielding a total of
\[
  \binom{15{,}432}{2} = 119{,}055{,}096 \text{ pairs.}
\]
At a throughput of 100 pair assessments per person-day, this corresponds to approximately 1,190,551 person-days --- \textbf{nearly 6,000 person-years of continuous expert effort}.  Figure~\ref{fig:analysis10c} plots this workload across all regulatory sources, illustrating both the absolute scale and the quadratic growth that makes each additional non-structure entry disproportionately costly.

Manual pairwise curation at this scale is not operationally feasible.  The only scalable alternative is to prevent the problem at the source: by requiring structure-resolvable identifiers and ontology-based representations in regulatory definitions wherever chemically possible, the population of entries requiring manual reconciliation can be structurally reduced rather than managed retrospectively.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_10c_workload_non_structured.pdf}
  \caption{Pairwise curation workload for all 15,432 non-structure entries across the combined ECHA regulatory dataset, broken down by source.  The total reaches approximately 1.2~million person-days --- nearly 6,000 person-years --- at an assumed throughput of 100 pair assessments per person-day.  Manual curation at this scale is not operationally feasible; interoperability must be addressed at the legislative source.}

\subsection{Composite Priority Scoring}
\label{sec:results-prioritisation}

The composite priority scoring model (Section~\ref{sec:prioritisation}) was applied to 17,518 structure-defined substances with at least one regulatory list membership. Scores combine regulatory breadth ($\text{list\_score}$) with biological hazard evidence from ChEBI ($\text{bio\_score}$).

\subsubsection{Top-Ranked Substances}

Figure~\ref{fig:analysis14a} presents the top~50 substances by priority score. Bisphenol A (2,2-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)propane; BPA) achieves the highest score of 33 ($\text{list\_score} = 30$, $\text{bio\_score} = 3$), appearing across 12 distinct regulatory sources including the Candidate List, Restriction List, Authorisation Process, and Harmonised Classification List. Dibutyl phthalate ranks second with a score of 29 ($\text{list\_score} = 26$, $\text{bio\_score} = 3$), present in 10 sources. The third-ranked substance, 2-(2H-benzotriazol-2-yl)-4,6-di-tert-pentylphenol (score 27), achieves a high regulatory score through simultaneous listing in eight sources including the POPs list and Authorisation List, but receives no biological hazard score, indicating a gap in current ChEBI role annotations for this compound.

\subsubsection{Biological Hazard Profile}

Of the 17,518 scored substances, 522 received a non-zero $\text{bio\_score}$ based on ChEBI role annotations. Figure~\ref{fig:analysis14d} shows the distribution of ChEBI biological activity labels across these substances. Herbicidal activity is the most prevalent annotation (139 substances; 26.6\% of scored entries), followed by allergen (76; 14.6\%) and carcinogenic agent (67; 12.8\%). The predominance of herbicides reflects the large contribution of the pesticides database to the total dataset. Among the high-hazard-weight categories (weight 3; CMR and persistent environmental contaminants), carcinogenic agent (67 substances), mutagen (35), genotoxin (20), teratogenic agent (16), persistent organic pollutant (16), and endocrine disruptor (5) together account for 30.6\% of biologically scored substances.
